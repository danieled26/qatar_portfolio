The Central Finding
Value for Money perception — not seat comfort, not catering — is the primary driver of recommendation intent. The propensity model surfaces this independently across both quantitative product scores and LLM-extracted free-text themes.
This is counter-intuitive for a premium carrier. It means product investment alone is insufficient — the quality improvement must be made legible to passengers at point of purchase and onboard. A great product that passengers cannot perceive as valuable will not move NPS.

The Advocacy Gap — Primary Finding
Passenger Sentiment Index 65/100 trails Recommendation Rate 71% by 6 points. A measurable segment recommends despite net negative sentiment — flying out of necessity, not loyalty.
These captive advocates are first to defect when a competitor enters the route. Standard NPS tracking misses this entirely because it only captures recommendation rate, not the emotional valence behind it. The propensity model's Would Rebook Signal identifies them.

Counter-Intuitive Anomaly — First Class Food
First Class food scores (avg 3.86/5) sit below the Economy average (3.75/5). The premium cabin delivers a worse food experience relative to passenger expectations than the standard cabin.
This is not a data artefact — it reflects a structural mismatch between First Class passenger expectations and actual delivery. High expectations + moderate delivery = lower perceived score, even when the absolute quality is higher than Economy.

The Investment Insight
Value for Money is the primary product lever — ranking 3rd and 5th independently as both an NLP free-text theme and a quantitative score. Ground Service ranks above Food & Beverage, Seat Comfort, and Wi-Fi, challenging the conventional assumption about catering as the headline investment priority.
Overall Rating (rank 1) and Would Rebook Signal (rank 2) are outcome proxies, not levers. The actionable investment framework begins at rank 3: Value theme → Sentiment Score → Value score → Ground Service. This is what the data says to invest in.

Strategic Recommendations — Ranked by Model Evidence
1

Value for Money Perception Primary Lever

Both the numeric score (rank 5) and NLP free-text theme (rank 3) surface independently in the model. Product investment is insufficient without a value narrative — passengers must be able to perceive the improvement as worth the fare.

2

Ground Service Underweighted Investment Area

Ranks above Food & Beverage, Seat Comfort, and Wi-Fi by XGBoost importance. Airport touchpoints set passenger expectations before boarding; low ground service scores create a negative prior that depresses all subsequent onboard ratings.

3

IFE Content Refresh — From Bi-Annual to Quarterly Medium Impact

IFE scores show a recurring mid-year dip correlated with stale content cycles. Passengers rating IFE ≤2/5 are significantly more likely to be NPS detractors. A quarterly refresh cycle is a low-cost, high-frequency intervention.

4

Captive Advocate Programme — Address the Advocacy Gap Retention Risk

The Would Rebook Signal (rank 2 by feature importance) identifies passengers who signal "maybe" — flying out of route necessity, not product loyalty. These are first to switch when a competitor enters. Standard NPS tracking is blind to this segment.

The Verdict — Roll Out the Treatment Menu
Economy class shows the largest lift (d = 0.850, Large effect). First Class lift is negligible — already at the satisfaction ceiling. Economy rollout delivers the highest ROI per dollar of meal cost uplift: 70% of passengers, largest absolute impact on NPS.
All six statistical tests — t-test, Mann-Whitney U, Chi-squared, Shapiro-Wilk, Cohen's d, 95% CI — confirm the same direction and magnitude. This is not a marginal result. It is a clear signal.
